Defining Reasoning

## Do Large Language Models Reason? Reasoning is a fundamental aspect of human thought, particularly evident in how we understand and use everyday language. When we encounter statements like "If A then B," our brains naturally work through the logic to draw conclusions. For instance, if "A" is true, we deduce that "B" must also be true (modus ponens). However, if we know "B" is not true (not-B), about half of us will conclude that "A" is not true either (modus tollens), while the other half might not draw a definite conclusion. In the context of generative AI, understanding these patterns of reasoning helps in developing models that can mimic human-like logical thought processes, enhancing their ability to generate coherent and contextually relevant responses. The idea of "reasoning" belongs to the larger group of processes known as "cognitive processes." These encompass a wide range of mental activities involved in acquiring, processing, and utilizing knowledge. Cognitive processes include perception, memory, attention, language, problem-solving, and decision-making, all of which are essential for understanding and interacting with the world. In the context of generative AI, simulating these cognitive processes, especially reasoning, is crucial for creating systems that can perform tasks requiring logical thought and decision-making, similar to how humans do. Judgment in the context of reasoning involves evaluating options, forming conclusions, and making decisions. This process relies on heuristics—mental shortcuts like attribute substitution, availability, representativeness, and anchoring—that help us make quick, efficient decisions. While these heuristics speed up reasoning, they can also lead to errors. In large language models, incorporating these aspects of human judgment can enhance their ability to simulate human-like decision-making and reasoning, making their responses more intuitive and relatable. https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/the-working-limitations-of-large-language-models/ Some people criticize LLMs and say “They are only predicting the next token”

Historically, automation has predominantly affected middle-paid production and clerical occupations, while new job creation has shifted towards high-paid professional roles and low-paid services. Technological advancements that complement occupational outputs and demand shocks are key drivers of new work emergence. However, automation innovations tend to erode labor demand, whereas augmentation innovations bolster it. In recent decades, there has been a notable increase in automation, especially in middle-skill occupations, while augmentation has been more prevalent in professional and technical fields. This trend suggests that while automation is accelerating, the creation of new, specialized work is not keeping pace, potentially leading to an imbalance in labor demand. The dual impact of augmentation and automation highlights the need for policies that encourage the creation of new work, which often commands higher wages and offers more opportunities for skill development. The future of labor in the context of rapid AI advancements remains uncertain. While AI has the potential to automate a wide range of tasks, it can also catalyze the emergence of novel specialties and complement existing skills. Early research indicates a broad spectrum of potential impacts, but definitive conclusions are still elusive. Understanding how AI innovations can be leveraged to foster both automation and augmentation will be crucial in shaping the future workforce and ensuring that new work opportunities are both valuable and accessible. * where do we go? Generative AI presents a dual opportunity to reshape roles in the workforce positively. Rather than solely focusing on automation and displacement, a more promising approach involves leveraging AI to complement human abilities, regardless of educational background. By augmenting workers' capabilities, from teachers to electricians, generative AI has the potential to elevate the quality of work, increase productivity, and ultimately boost wages. This shift towards human-complementary AI requires not only changes in technological development but also shifts in corporate practices towards fostering innovation that enhances workers' skills and expertise. Ultimately, embracing this path could lead to a more equitable distribution of opportunities and resources within the labor market.
