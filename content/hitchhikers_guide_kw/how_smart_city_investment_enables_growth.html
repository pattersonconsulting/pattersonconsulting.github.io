How Smart City Investment Enables Regional Economic Growth

By: Josh Patterson

Introduction, state core thesis

  • people are moving to urban areas for opportunity
  • trend: industrial revolution, structural transformation
  • city managers need to enable growth

Investment in smart city technology is a way for cities to increase knowledge work productivity and enable economic growth of their region. Cities can use sensors and algorithms to gather data on the operations of a city. Converting this raw data into information:

  • enables more informed urban planning
  • enhances mobility intelligence
  • supports data-driven insurance optimization

By making previously inaccessible city data usable, smart city technology empowers stakeholders to reduce costs, improve operational efficiency, and increase the overall productivity of city systems.

Cities actively pursue growth as a strategic imperative to enhance their economic vitality and improve the quality of life for their residents. Growth attracts talent, increases tax revenue, and fosters innovation by creating a dynamic ecosystem of businesses, educational institutions, and cultural amenities. The promise of better employment prospects, a higher standard of living, and greater access to education drives population inflows, reinforcing the city’s role as a hub of opportunity. This growth cycle, in turn, fuels further development and investment, positioning cities as central engines of economic activity.

Roman aqueducts enabled technical change by providing a reliable and large-scale water supply to cities, which supported population growth, improved sanitation, and boosted productivity. With more water, cities could sustain public baths, fountains, and sewage systems, improve public health, and support industries like tanning, dyeing, and milling. This infrastructure reduced the labor needed to fetch water manually and allowed urban economies to grow, specialize, and innovate—key features of technical change that fueled the expansion and prosperity of Roman cities.

segue from physical infrastructure to knowledge work, physical infrastructure largely settled, knowledge work infrastructure now the competitive space

productivity as mechanics gear ratios

However, urban growth presents significant challenges that must be addressed to ensure long-term sustainability. Historically, cities have faced impediments such as pandemics, natural disasters like earthquakes and fires, and the strain of rapid population increases. Effective urban planning, traffic management, and the enforcement of building codes and compliance standards are essential to mitigating these risks. Additionally, cities must manage the increasing fiscal burden associated with scaling public services and infrastructure. Proactive governance and strategic investment are critical to balancing growth with resilience and operational efficiency.

The Economics of the Smart City

What are we after? (economics of smart city)

  • need economic growth to compete for residents
  • however, we need to scale operations of managing city
  • benefit of more revenue only works if costs scale sub-linearly with population

Population growth and regional economic development are closely intertwined, as evidenced by the historical and modern evolution of cities. Urban centers—defined by dense populations and self-governance—have historically relied on surplus food production to support non-agricultural labor and complex infrastructure. The Industrial Revolution marked a turning point, accelerating urbanization through rural-to-urban migration driven by labor demand in emerging industries.

As healthcare and agriculture improved, the global population quadrupled from 1920 to 2022, fueling unprecedented urban expansion. Today, the largest cities are typically found in the world’s strongest economies, indicating that sustained economic growth supports urban concentration, while the scale and vitality of cities reflect broader regional prosperity.

Smart cities are increasingly leveraging data platforms and applied AI to improve financial performance and operational resilience. Three core models of utility help frame this economic transformation:

  1. user-based insurance, where real-time monitoring and compliance can reduce a city's insurance costs
  2. applications that directly save money, such as prioritizing infrastructure repairs
  3. solutions that reduce operational risk, including retrospective analysis of adverse events

These models offer measurable returns by aligning technological investments with fiscal efficiency and risk mitigation.

city operational costs should scale sub-linearly to population size of region (econ of scale) --- no benefit to added revenue if operational costs scale at the same rate

As cities scale, they face rising infrastructure complexity and mounting fiscal overhead. Smart technologies provide a path to manage this growth more sustainably—lowering costs through automation, optimizing maintenance, and enabling proactive planning. By integrating these tools, cities can not only improve service delivery but also enhance their insurability, resilience, and economic viability—key metrics that are increasingly relevant to insurers evaluating urban risk profiles.

The Challenges of Municipal Knowledge Work

What are the main obstacles?

  • traffic
  • urban planning, housing, codes (fire, disease)
  • educational opportunities

As urban environments become more complex and climate risks intensify, cities must develop a deeper, data-driven understanding of critical systems such as transit, housing, utilities, and environmental quality. This requires comprehensive insights into variables like population movement, water and power usage, air quality, and infrastructure utilization. A lack of precise, real-time data hampers the ability of municipalities to adapt to evolving conditions—an issue highlighted by the growing disconnect between catastrophe models and the realities of climate change. Without this foundational knowledge, cities struggle to implement effective building codes, plan resilient infrastructure, and ensure operational continuity.

Urban planning is blind without the right information. urban planning more effectively manages traffic and provides better housing opportunities for citizens

To meet these challenges, municipalities must invest in the ability to measure and analyze traffic patterns, environmental metrics, human activity, and the condition of physical assets. Doing so enables a range of high-value applications, from dynamic road tolling and traffic optimization to enhanced public safety and environmental monitoring. This evolution in municipal knowledge work supports smarter policy, more agile operations, and risk-informed urban planning. For insurers, improved municipal intelligence translates into more accurate risk assessment and greater alignment between exposure models and on-the-ground realities.

How CUIP Smart City Research Enables Regional Growth

How do we get over the main obstacle?

  • knowledge work: city operations, urban planning, transportation, codes, compliance
  • information: data platform
  • productivity: algorithms, AI

The Center for Urban Informatics and Progress (CUIP) is advancing practical smart city solutions by focusing on enabling cities through data, not hype. Their approach moves beyond futuristic concepts toward grounded, high-impact use cases—such as advanced mobility intelligence and data-driven insurance optimization. By collecting and refining data into actionable information, CUIP empowers cities to make smarter decisions that improve planning, operations, and financial performance.

The emphasis is on supporting municipal knowledge work through technology to support urban planning, reduce traffic congestion, and lower insurance risk.

At the core of CUIP’s strategy is its Smart City Test Bed, where real-world city operations are instrumented with intersection sensors and integrated into a central data platform. This system transforms raw sensor data into usable insights, giving city planners and operators access to information they previously lacked. The result is more informed urban planning, better traffic and mobility management, and opportunities to lower insurance premiums through improved risk visibility.

These capabilities collectively drive down operational costs, boost efficiency, and enhance the productivity of city systems—creating real economic and strategic value for municipalities and their stakeholders, including insurers.
