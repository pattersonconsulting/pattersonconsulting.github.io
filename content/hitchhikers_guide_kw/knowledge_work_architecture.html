Knowledge Work Architecture

By: Josh Patterson

In the age of generative AI, many people forget that the humble data platform is the foundation for information transformation in today's organizations.

Logical Layers of a Data Platform

At Patterson Consulting we model an organization's data platform as 3 logical layers:

  1. The knowledge work layer
  2. The information layer
  3. The infrastructure layer

Obviously this is different than many other shop's platform architectures; We went to this logical model (based on our industry experience) because honestly most of the industry had run the idea into the ground with 100's of boxes tacked onto a diagram (e.g., all the "modern data platform architecture" diagrams).

If we accept the framing that:

“insurance/finance/[your domain] is knowledge work”

and

“Knowledge work is information transformation”

Then we can also accept the idea that companies can improve efficiency by either accelerating work processes or coordinating teams more efficiently.

From that perspective, any data platform investment or change should consider:

  • “How does this affect line of business efficiency?”
  • “How well does our platform convert raw input data into information that produces revenue?”

By starting with a logical definition of a data platform we can avoid any specific technology, platform, or vendor bias in the planning or review phase of data platfrom architecture. This is an advantage because many times an organization will get sold by a vendor on the idea that their platform "will do it all" and yet the technology never meets this threshold after much investment.

So let's begin with "what is the knowledge work layer?"

The Knowledge Work Layer

Beyond workflow efficiency, data platforms enhance organizational coordination by structuring and synchronizing knowledge work. Just as early civilizations used stone tablets to record transactions and calendars to organize activities, today’s data platforms centralize information, enabling seamless collaboration and decision-making across teams. Without this capability, these systems risk becoming mere technical overhead, failing to deliver the competitive advantages necessary to improve key business outcomes.

The Information Layer

The Information Layer serves as the foundation for ensuring data consistency, accessibility, and strategic value within an organization. As data sources grow in complexity and business users rely on various BI tools and self-service analytics, maintaining a single logical source of truth becomes critical. This layer aggregates and standardizes data, enabling knowledge work tools to function effectively while ensuring consistency across metrics. By aligning with the company’s broader data strategy, the Information Layer helps transform raw data into actionable insights, supporting decision-making and driving business objectives.

The Information Layer abstracts the complexity of raw data management, enabling business users to focus on generating insights rather than dealing with data engineering challenges. By integrating data from multiple sources into a unified data store and model, it ensures consistency, accessibility, and efficiency in decision-making. Similar to how a restaurant menu allows customers to select meals without needing to understand the cooking process, the Information Layer provides a curated, ready-to-use view of data, accelerating knowledge work and ensuring that teams can derive business value without being burdened by underlying technical complexities.

The Medallion Architecture (commonly associated with data lakehouses, such as on Databricks) organizes data into different layers: Bronze, Silver, and Gold. Each layer serves a specific purpose in ensuring data quality, integrity, and accessibility.

Bronze Layer

  • Raw Data Ingestion: The Bronze layer stores raw, unprocessed data, often ingested directly from source systems (e.g., CSV files, JSON logs, streaming data).
  • Minimal Transformation: Data in the Bronze layer typically undergoes minimal or no transformation—there may be basic cleaning (such as removing corrupted records) or partitioning for storage efficiency.
  • Data Retention: Because it is raw, the Bronze layer serves as a reliable “landing zone” or system of record, preserving the original state of the data should it need to be reprocessed or audited.

Silver Layer

  • Cleansed and Refined Data: The Silver layer refines Bronze data by applying data cleaning and normalization steps (e.g., removing duplicates, standardizing formats, fixing malformed records, or filling missing values).
  • Data Validation: Data quality checks and validations happen in this layer, ensuring that only data meeting predefined quality thresholds moves forward.
  • Intermediate Business Logic: This layer starts to incorporate certain business rules or transformations needed for advanced analytics, but it still retains a relatively broad scope.

Gold Layer

  • Curated, Business-Ready Data: The Gold layer contains data sets that have gone through all necessary transformations, making them ready for dashboards, advanced analytics, or machine learning models.
  • Aggregations and Summaries: Common operations here include aggregations, dimensional modeling, and calculations of key performance indicators (KPIs), effectively preparing data to meet specific business requirements.
  • High Trust, Low Volume: Compared to the raw data in Bronze, the Gold layer is typically smaller in size but holds the highest data quality and is trusted for direct business consumption.

This multi-layer approach improves data quality, ensures trust, and provides an efficient framework for data reuse. It is a flexible model that can be adapted to various use cases and technology platforms.
