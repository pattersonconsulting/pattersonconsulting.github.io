The Agency Trap

By: Josh Patterson

Every few months, someone prominent announces that AGI — Artificial General Intelligence — is close. Eighteen months away. Five years. A decade at most. When it arrives, the argument goes, it will render most human labor economically obsolete. Not some labor. Most of it. The knowledge worker, the analyst, the underwriter, the lawyer — replaced, not elevated.

This argument has a structure problem. It isn't wrong because AGI is impossible. It's wrong because it smuggles in a series of assumptions — about accountability, legal standing, moral responsibility, and social legitimacy — and treats them as already resolved. They aren't. And they won't be resolved automatically by capability improvements, however large.

This is the Agency Trap: assuming that if an AI system can perform a task, it can also own the task — bearing the accountability, authority, and institutional standing that human workers carry. Capability and agency are not the same thing. The argument that AGI ends human labor collapses them.

The Target Keeps Moving

Start with the claim itself. "AGI" has no agreed definition. Depending on who you ask, it means: human-level performance across benchmarks, general reasoning in novel domains, conscious experience, or recursive self-improvement. Each definition implies a different threshold and a different timeline.

François Chollet, who developed the ARC benchmark specifically to test general reasoning, argues that current AI systems demonstrate impressive performance on trained distributions but fail to show the fluid generalization that "general" intelligence requires.[1] Melanie Mitchell's analysis reaches a similar conclusion: AI systems consistently solve the specific problem they were trained on while struggling with adjacent problems that humans handle trivially.[2]

The capability gains are real. The generality is not — yet. And "around the corner" is not a timeline; it's a rhetorical posture. The goalposts move with the technology. This is the same pattern that has played out every time a wave of AI capability gains arrived — the definition of "general intelligence" quietly shifts forward to a horizon that hasn't been reached.

Capability Without Agency: The Hard Problems Remain

Grant the strongest version of the argument. Stipulate a system with human-level or greater task performance across all cognitive domains. Does that resolve the labor question?

No. Three problems survive any capability improvement.

Accountability cannot be automated away. When an AI system causes harm — a wrong coverage decision, a flawed legal brief, a misdiagnosis — someone is responsible. Not the system. The Restatement (Third) of Agency is clear: an agent must be capable of bearing legal liability for their own conduct.[3] Software cannot be sued, fined, or imprisoned. All legal consequences fall back on the humans who designed, deployed, and authorized the system. You haven't replaced the human worker; you've obscured them in the accountability chain. Raji and colleagues have documented this empirically: when AI systems produce harmful outputs, accountability diffuses across developers, deployers, and users in ways that courts and regulators find increasingly unacceptable.[4]

Moral responsibility requires more than performance. Harry Frankfurt's foundational analysis of agency argues that what distinguishes persons from mechanisms is the capacity for second-order desires: reflecting on one's motivations, endorsing or rejecting them, and acting from values genuinely made one's own.[5] Iason Gabriel, in a careful analysis of AI alignment, concludes that no current architecture — and no architecture whose design principles we currently understand — produces genuine values in this sense.[6] A system that optimizes is not a system that deliberates. These are not the same thing. A system that cannot deliberate cannot bear moral responsibility. A system that cannot bear moral responsibility cannot replace a worker who can.

Institutional legitimacy is a social choice, not a technical milestone. Even if a system could act rather than react — pursuing its own goals autonomously across time — this creates new problems rather than solving existing ones. If it has genuine preferences, the alignment problem becomes the central issue, not a footnote.[7] And social legitimacy — the acceptance of a system's decisions as valid and binding — is granted by humans through institutions, not conferred by capability. Regulators in insurance, medicine, and finance have made this explicit: decisions affecting others require documented human accountability. This reflects a political and ethical choice that will not dissolve because a system gets smarter.

What If It Really Could Act?

The deeper version of the AGI argument sometimes acknowledges the accountability problem and responds: but what if legal frameworks adapted to recognize AI agents as legal persons?

This is a serious argument. It is also one that creates as many problems as it solves. Legal personhood for non-human entities exists — corporations have it — but it is constructed and constrained by human institutions, not emergent from capability. And Nick Bostrom's treatment of the question is worth taking seriously: a genuinely autonomous system with its own goals is not obviously a tool any longer.[8] It may have interests that conflict with human interests. The question of who it serves becomes existential rather than operational.

The "AGI replaces all labor" argument works only if you accept the full stack of assumptions beneath it: that capability generalizes to genuine agency, that accountability can be automated away, that moral responsibility is unnecessary or transferable, that legal frameworks will adapt smoothly, and that the alignment problem is solved or irrelevant. Each assumption requires an independent argument. None is obviously true.

The Persistent Minimum

What the history of knowledge work actually shows — from the spreadsheet to the relational database to the current wave of LLM tools — is that automation transforms the composition of human labor, not its necessity. This is the same pattern the mechanical loom established in the textile industry two centuries ago: mechanize the repeatable structural operations, and what remains for human hands is everything the machine can't do — judgment, quality control, exception handling, and the decisions that have no established pattern before you start.[9]

The same dynamic holds for cognitive labor. Each time a new information tool arrives — whether it's the filing cabinet, the spreadsheet, the database, or the large language model — the routine synthesis work shifts to the new tool and the human's time reorients toward the work that requires genuine judgment. The output ceiling rises. The demand for skilled judgment work increases, not decreases, because the tool expands what the organization can address. That is not a prediction. It is the documented pattern from every prior wave.

The Minimum Viable Person persists at every level of automation, because capability without accountability is not a replacement. It is a tool.

The Agency Trap is the belief that capability is enough. It isn't. It never was.

  3. American Law Institute. Restatement (Third) of Agency § 1.01 (2006). An agent must be capable of bearing legal liability for their own wrongful conduct.
