The Minimum Viable Person

By: Josh Patterson

The word "agent" is doing a lot of work right now.

In a typical vendor demo, an AI agent reads a contract, flags three risk clauses, drafts a response, and sends it to legal — without a human touching it. The audience draws a conclusion: the lawyer is next. Why keep the person?

This conclusion moves too fast, and it does so because it misuses a word.

Agency Has a Precise Meaning

Outside of technology marketing, "agency" carries specific weight in two traditions that matter here.

In law, agency is the capacity to act on behalf of another with legal accountability. The Restatement (Third) of Agency defines an agent as someone who can act on a principal's behalf and bear liability for their own conduct.[1] An agent can be sued. An agent signs things that mean something.

In philosophy, agency is the capacity for self-directed action. Harry Frankfurt argues that genuine agency requires will — specifically, "second-order desires," the capacity to reflect on one's motivations and endorse or reject them.[2] A genuine agent doesn't just respond; it governs its responses against values it has made its own.

An LLM meets neither definition. It cannot be sued. It has no will in Frankfurt's sense. It responds to prompts it didn't write, toward goals it didn't set.

Calling an LLM pipeline an "agent" is useful technical shorthand. Treating it as a claim about genuine agency is where the replacement argument breaks down.

Three Reasons the Replacement Argument Fails

First: Accountability doesn't disappear — it becomes invisible.

When an AI system produces a harmful output, "who is responsible?" still demands a human answer. Someone designed it. Someone deployed it. Someone authorized it to act. The accountability chain doesn't break because the system acted autonomously; it becomes harder to trace — which is not a feature, it's a liability.

This is why regulators are explicit: AI-assisted decisions require documented human accountability. The NAIC's 2023 Model Bulletin establishes that the insurer — meaning identifiable humans — retains full governance responsibility for every AI-assisted coverage determination.[3] You cannot eliminate the person. You can only obscure them, which is worse.

Second: Reacting is not the same as acting.

Genuine autonomy requires that an entity set its own goals and direct its own actions toward them. LLMs respond. They do not initiate, persist across sessions, or hold positions over time without continuous human scaffolding. The "self" in self-directed is missing.

Dennett's "intentional stance" is useful here: we describe a thermostat as if it wants the room to be warm, and that language aids prediction.[4] But the thermostat doesn't want anything. The accountability that follows from genuine trying — responsibility, ownership of error — belongs only to humans in the system.

Third: The work that would make agents most valuable is the work that most resists them.

The tasks that define the judgment and strategic layers of knowledge work — genuinely novel situations, conflicting signals, decisions requiring trust — are precisely what automation handles least reliably. Polanyi observed that skilled practitioners "can know more than they can tell": they apply judgment they cannot fully articulate, drawing on tacit knowledge that resists explicit specification.[5] Autor named this Polanyi's Paradox — the structural reason non-routine cognitive tasks resist automation even as computing power grows.[6]

An AI agent can produce output that looks like a judgment. It cannot bear responsibility for being wrong in the way judgment requires. The analyst who reviews the agent's output and signs the report is doing something the agent cannot — taking epistemic and institutional ownership of a conclusion.

What Remains: The Minimum Viable Person

In product development, the Minimum Viable Product is the smallest version of something that still works.

The Minimum Viable Person is the irreducible human function that remains when automation is maximized: someone who can be held accountable, someone who can exercise genuine judgment in novel situations, and someone whose authorization gives the AI's output institutional legitimacy.

This person's role is transformed — as in every prior mechanization wave, from bookkeepers who became analysts to weavers who became quality directors. The agent handles the threads. The person decides what the garment is for, and answers for it.

Organizations that ask "how many workers can the agents replace?" will get the wrong answer. The right question is: what does the Minimum Viable Person in this role actually do? Build around that, and the agent becomes leverage. Ignore it, and the accountability you thought you eliminated will surface somewhere you didn't expect.
