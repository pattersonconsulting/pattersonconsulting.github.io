What is Data Engineering?

How does data engineering evolve in the age of generative AI?

Author: Josh Patterson

A Definition for Data Engineering

Data engineering is the discipline focused on designing, constructing, and maintaining the systems that manage the reliable and efficient processing of large volumes of data. This encompasses tasks such as collecting, storing, transforming, and organizing data to make it accessible and usable for analysis, reporting, and decision-making.

By working with various tools and technologies, data engineers build robust data pipelines, ensure data quality, and support the needs of data scientists, analysts, and other stakeholders within an organization. Essentially, data engineering provides the crucial infrastructure for data-driven insights and strategic decisions.

A data engineer’s role is multifaceted, reflecting the diverse needs of an organization. They are responsible for storing, extracting, transforming, loading, aggregating, and validating data. This involves building efficient data pipelines, ensuring data adheres to governance rules, and understanding the best storage and query options based on the data’s structure. For instance, an enterprise might use AWS for cloud services, requiring different strategies for handling structured, unstructured, normalized, or denormalized data, as well as row or columnar data formats. Key considerations include the nature of the data (e.g., key/value-based) and the complexity of relationships within the data, influencing how it is ingested, processed, and curated.

For application developers, data engineering focuses on moving data in bulk rather than transaction by transaction, addressing a common pain point in many organizations. For upper management, it can be likened to constructing a solid foundation necessary for all data-related projects and initiatives. The versatility of a data engineer, much like a Swiss army knife, allows them to adapt to various roles and responsibilities, ensuring that systems have reliable, timely, and well-controlled data. Their work enables the organization to derive meaningful insights and solve complex problems, making data engineering a strategic initiative for any data-driven enterprise.

Before we get into the 3 phases of data engineering and then further in this article the evolution of data engineering, I want to talk for a minute about where great data products and ideas come from with respect to the data engineering proces. I want to talk about the ideas introduced in “The Lab and the Factory”.

The Lab and the Factory

In the context of data engineering, the concepts of “The Lab and The Factory” (from the original article “To Work with Data, You Need a Lab and a Factory” by Thomas C. Redman and Bill Sweeney in Harvard Business Review, 2013) illustrate two distinct yet interconnected phases of data handling and processing.

The “Lab” represents the experimental phase where data scientists and analysts explore data, develop hypotheses, and create new ideas, machine learning models, or new data products. This environment is characterized by agility, flexibility, and spartan use of resources. This allows for rapid iteration and the testing of various approaches and ideas. Many new and emerge data products come from the lab, where engineers test out an idea in a Jupyter notebook or a python script with a data sample. However, many times the engineer needs to quickly throw together some tools or code, and its often a jumbled mess — which is ok because many of the ideas will fail.

On the other hand, the “Factory” refers to the production phase where data workflows and models developed in the “Lab” are operationalized and scaled for regular use. This involves automating data pipelines, ensuring data quality, and optimizing performance to handle large volumes of data efficiently. In the “Factory”, data engineering emphasizes stability, scalability, and reliability, ensuring that the insights and models produced in the “Lab” can be consistently applied in real-world scenarios.

By bridging these two phases, data engineering enables organizations to transition seamlessly from experimentation to deployment, driving sustained value from their data assets. However, this is not always easy because when we want to move one of those precious few good ideas that make it out of the “Lab”, the code or notebook is not ready to be a full productionized data pipeline or process. This is where a good data engineering practice and team shine. Let’s dig into what the 3 phases of the productionized data engineering process look like.

The 3 Key Phases of Data Engineering

Data engineering involves three crucial phases:

  • data collection
  • data transformation
  • data analysis and integration

The first phase, data collection, focuses on gathering data from diverse internal and external sources, which can be ingested in real-time or in batch processes. Ensuring this data is cataloged properly is essential for maintaining security, governance, and compliance. Additionally, monitoring the quality and performance of the collected data is vital to prevent issues downstream and to maintain the integrity of the datasets.

The second phase, data transformation, involves cleansing and enriching the data to enhance its value and usability. This phase includes modeling the data to suit the specific needs of various stakeholders, making it more accessible and relevant. Effective management of workflows for scheduling, auditing, and performance is critical during this phase to ensure smooth and efficient operations.

The final phase, data analysis and integration, leverages tools for analytics, machine learning, and AI to extract insights from the data. This phase also focuses on serving data to applications, processes, or people, and governing the catalog of data, dictionaries, and mappings to maintain an organized and accessible data environment. In more recent times this involves integrating data into systems that support generative AI as well.

Together, these phases enable a seamless flow from raw data to actionable insights, driving informed decision-making and innovation.

Why is Data Engineering Important?

Data engineering serves as the backbone of any data-driven operation, facilitating the collection, storage, processing, and analysis of vast quantities of information. Here are three key reasons why data engineering holds paramount importance:

  1. Data Quality Assurance
  2. Scalability and Performance Optimization
  3. Enablement of Advanced Analytics and AI

Data engineers play a crucial role in efficiently collecting data from various sources, storing it in data lakes or Kafka topics, and determining optimal ways to join these datasets. They ensure data pipelines have accurate inputs and outputs, often involving reconciliation and validation processes. By utilizing monitoring tools and SRE practices, they maintain continuous data flow and keep information current.

Ultimately, data engineers add significant value by automating and optimizing complex systems, transforming raw data into accessible and actionable business assets.

How Do Organizations Derive Value from Data Engineering?

Organizations derive immense value from data engineering by efficiently aggregating data from diverse sources like ERP systems, supply chains, and third-party vendors into centralized data lakes, cloud data warehouses, or semantic layers.

Data engineers optimize the joining of these datasets and build robust pipelines that ensure seamless data flow and availability for business intelligence tools. They maintain data accuracy through reconciliation and validation, and use monitoring tools and SRE practices to keep data pipelines up-to-date. Ultimately, data engineers transform complex data systems into accessible, valuable business assets through automation and optimization.

Data engineering supports business teams, product owners, and data science teams by providing a reliable infrastructure for data management. It ensures data is aggregated, validated, and stored centrally, allowing business teams to quickly access accurate information for decision-making. Product owners benefit from up-to-date and accessible data for tracking metrics and improving products. Data scientists rely on the robust data pipelines maintained by data engineers, freeing them to focus on analysis and model building. Overall, data engineering transforms complex data systems into valuable, actionable assets for these key stakeholders.

What are Key Technologies in Data Engineering?

Key technologies in data engineering include:

  • Data Collection: Fivetran, etc
  • Data Warehousing: Amazon Redshift, Google BigQuery, and Snowflake
  • Data Storage: Including relational databases (e.g., MySQL, PostgreSQL), NoSQL databases (e.g., MongoDB, Cassandra), and cloud storage solutions (e.g., Amazon S3, Google Cloud Storage).
  • Data Pipeline Orchestration: Tools such as DBT, Apache Airflow, Apache Oozie, and AWS Glue

These technologies work together to enable data engineers to efficiently collect, process, store, analyze, and visualize large volumes of data, ultimately supporting data-driven decision-making and innovation within organizations.

Key data transformation technologies in data engineering enable the processing, manipulation, and enrichment of data to prepare it for analysis, reporting, or other downstream tasks. These technologies help transform raw data into a format that is suitable for consumption by users or applications. Some of the key data transformation technologies include:

  • Azure Synapse
  • AWS Redshift
  • Google BigQuery
  • Apache Spark / PySpark
  • Pandas / Python ecosystem

These data transformation technologies offer a range of capabilities for processing and manipulating data to meet the needs of different use cases and requirements in data engineering. They play a critical role in enabling organizations to extract value from their data assets and drive data-driven decision-making and innovation.

Orchestration technologies in data engineering enable the coordination and automation of various data processing tasks and workflows. These technologies help manage complex data pipelines, ensuring efficient and reliable execution. These orchestration technologies offer capabilities for managing the complexity of data workflows, ensuring scalability, fault tolerance, and reliability in data processing operations. They play a crucial role in enabling efficient data engineering practices and supporting the development of data-driven applications and analytics solutions.

Why Are Cloud Platforms Popular for Data Engineering?

Cloud platforms are popular for data engineering due to their scalability, flexibility, and cost-effectiveness. They allow data engineers to easily scale resources up or down based on demand, handle large data volumes, and experiment with various configurations without upfront hardware investments. The pay-as-you-go pricing model ensures cost efficiency by charging only for the resources used, eliminating the need for significant capital expenditures.

Additionally, cloud platforms offer managed services that simplify infrastructure management, allowing data engineers to focus on optimizing data pipelines. Their global reach reduces latency for distributed applications, and robust security features ensure data protection and regulatory compliance. Seamless integration with other services and continuous innovation provide data engineers with the latest tools and technologies, making cloud platforms a versatile and forward-thinking choice for data engineering across diverse industries.

However, data engineering did not start in the cloud. I believe to understand data engineering you need to be aware of the evolution of data engineering.

The Evolution of Data Engineering

To better understand the role of data engineering and the types of engineers involved, I want to take a quick look at how the space has evolved over the past 20 years. In that timespan there have been 4 major eras:

  • pre-2010: “Traditional RDBMS”, Stored Procedures, SQL, Informatica, etc
  • 2010-2015: “Big data era”, hadoop, MapReduce, Oozie
  • 2016-2020: “Migration to the cloud”, data pipelines on cloud platforms, rise of python, re-birth of SQL
  • 2021-2024: “The Semantic Era”, DBT, semantic layer, supporting Gen AI

pre-2010: Traditional RDBMS

In this era tools such traditional RDBMS systems (Oracle, MySQL, SQL Server, etc) and distributed database systems (e.g., Teradata, Vertica) worked with tooling such as Informatica to build early data pipelines. SQL was the dominant data transformation language.

2010-2015: Big Data

This era was defined by the growth of “big data”, Hadoop, distributed systems, and computation at scale. In the “big data”-era, data engineering was more commonly referred to as ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) and there was a movement away from SQL towards other methods such as MapReduce (note: projects such as Apache Hive still allowed a SQL-variant to run on MapReduce). Apache Oozie was the go-to workflow orchestrator during the Hadoop era. Data pipelines for machine learning use cases began to emerge at the tail end of this era.

2016-2020: Migration to the Cloud

Cloud costs came down, python continued to rise in prominence in the data space, and SQL finds a re-birth with the introduction of cloud platform data warehouses. Companies began to test out moving some of their data pipelines to the cloud.

Machine learning and data science comes to prominence with the rise of deep learning, GPUs, and the python ecosystem. In this era feature engineering becomes a practice that dovetails into the outputs of data engineering for machine learning projects.

Maxime Beauchemin wrote a key article on the role of the data engineering in 2017 called “The Rise of the Data Engineer” where he defines Data Engineering as:

“data engineers build tools, infrastructure, frameworks, and services. In fact, it’s arguable that data engineering is much closer to software engineering than it is to a data science”

It was also in this era that DBT emerges as an orchestration tool built on SQL, rising with the new wave of cloud data warehouses. Further, there is a shift from ETL towards ELT that reflects a broader evolution in data engineering practices, driven by the need for more efficient and scalable data processing solutions.

2021-2024: The Semantic Era

In our current era we continue to see companies build out their data platforms and data products on the cloud in the generative AI age. New trends we see are “data modeling in the semantic layer” (e.g., cube.dev’s semantic layer) to abstract certain logic out of the BI-layer such that other tools can use a unified data model.

This centralized data model is also useful in the generative AI era where we can allow agents to use business logic over data models specific to the organization. There is also associated semantic metadata that is useful for reasoning in large language models (e.g., “generative AI”). These new Gen AI use cases need data and metadata in a way that is different from traditional data science and machine learning. This information is closer in structure to traditional data warehouse data models yet have their own specific data structures and query methods to power semantic Gen AI applications. This new area of data engineering is why I call this era “The Semantic Era” of data engineering.

Now that we understand what data engineering is and how it has evolved, let’s take a look at how data teams are structured.

Data Engineers in Data Teams

There are different roles on modern data teams in larger organizations. These roles are commonly:

  • data engineer
  • analytics engineer
  • data analyst
  • platform engineer
  • ml engineer
  • generative AI engineer
  • data scientist

They each have different yet overlapping skills.

I want to start out by digging into what a data engineer does in 2024.

Data Engineer

A data engineer is like the backbone of a thriving ecosystem, ensuring every component works harmoniously to sustain the whole. Data engineers primarily focus on designing, building, and maintaining data infrastructure and pipelines to support data-driven applications and analytics. Their responsibilities include data ingestion, transformation, storage, and retrieval. They also ensure data quality, reliability, and availability.

Data engineers typically possess skills in programming languages like Python, SQL, and Scala. They are proficient in working with big data technologies such as Apache Spark, Hadoop, and Kafka. They also have expertise in data modeling, ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) processes, and database management.

Their main objective is to enable efficient data processing, analysis, and visualization, ultimately supporting decision-making and business insights.

What Tasks are Handled by Data Engineers?

In smaller companies, data engineering roles often extend to encompass platform engineering (DevOps) responsibilities, managing the organization’s data infrastructure. This includes setting up and maintaining platforms like Hadoop, Hive, HBase, and Spark. By handling both data engineering and infrastructure operations, these professionals ensure that data systems are robust and efficient, laying the groundwork for reliable data processing and analysis.

In larger companies the tasks are split up between roles such as the platform engineer, machine learning, engineer, data analyts, etc roles. In larger companies the data engineer focuses more on the specific data pipelines that load, transform, and analyze the data as opposed to building out the platform or performing platform migrations.

Data Engineer vs Data Scientists

Data engineers and data scientists play distinct yet complementary roles in the data ecosystem. Data engineers focus on building and maintaining the infrastructure needed for data collection, storage, and processing. They implement data governance practices to ensure data quality and accuracy, and they perform large-scale transformations to prepare data for analysis. Data scientists, on the other hand, use this well-prepared data to create models and extract insights. They conduct complex analyses and develop algorithms, often requiring sophisticated mathematical and statistical techniques that go beyond the typical scope of a data engineer’s work. In scenarios where these models need to be productionized, machine learning engineers bridge the gap, turning prototypes into scalable solutions. This division of labor ensures that each expert can leverage their specialized skills to contribute to the overall success of data-driven projects.

Data Analysts

A data analyst is a professional who examines large and complex sets of data to identify patterns, trends, and insights that can be used to inform business decisions. They use statistical and analytical techniques such as regression analysis, hypothesis testing, and data visualization to extract insights from data. They are skilled in programming languages such as SQL, Python, or R, and data manipulation tools such as Excel or Tableau.

Data analysts are responsible for collecting and cleaning data, preparing it for analysis, and presenting their findings to stakeholders through reports or visualizations. They play a critical role in informing business decisions by providing insights based on data analysis. Overall, data analysts are highly analytical professionals who are responsible for extracting insights from data and communicating their findings to stakeholders.

Analytics Engineer

An analytics engineer bridges the gap between data engineering and data analysis, providing clean, well-modeled data sets that empower end users to perform their own analyses. Unlike data analysts, who focus primarily on interpreting data, analytics engineers spend their time transforming, testing, deploying, and documenting data. They apply software engineering best practices, such as version control and continuous integration, to maintain a reliable analytics code base. This role is crucial for ensuring that data is accessible, accurate, and well-organized, enabling businesses to make informed decisions efficiently and effectively.

Machine Learning Engineer

A machine learning engineer sits at the crossroads of data engineering and data science, equipped with robust mathematical skills and practical knowledge of data structures and algorithms. These engineers are adept at using data engineering tools and frameworks within big data ecosystems to apply data models developed by data scientists. Their key role is to productionize these models, ensuring they can handle and process large volumes of real-time data efficiently. This involves creating high-performance environments for data ingestion and computation, often managing terabytes of data to drive actionable business insights. Their work is essential in making advanced analytics scalable and operational within an organization, blending technical precision with innovative application.

Generative AI Engineer

I’m going to introduce a new member to the data team called the “generative AI engineer”. The generative AI engineer is similar in idea to the machine learning engineer (uses output of data engineering to build new products) but is closer to a more traditional software engineer.

A generative AI engineer uses tools such as LangChain to integrate data products from data models and the semantic layer into prompts via prompt engineering to reason over with large language models.

A generative AI engineer understands retrieval augmented generation (RAG) and how to blend the reasoning power of large language models with enterprise knowledge in the data warehouse.

These new generative AI applications use several task workflows of the above prompt engineering pattern to integrate enterprise data into natural language interfaces, scenario analyses, and workflow automations.

Generative AI Engineer == Data Enginer + Prompt Engineer

Make no mistake, “Generative AI Engineer” is that next hot job title.

Platform Engineer

Platform Engineers (e.g., “cloud dev ops”) focus on automating and managing the deployment, scaling, and monitoring of applications and infrastructure within cloud environments. Their responsibilities include configuring and managing cloud resources, implementing CI/CD pipelines, automating infrastructure provisioning, and ensuring high availability and reliability of cloud-based applications.

Cloud DevOps professionals need expertise in cloud platforms such as AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud Platform. They should be familiar with infrastructure as code (IaC) tools like Terraform or AWS CloudFormation, containerization technologies like Docker and Kubernetes, and continuous integration/continuous deployment (CI/CD) practices.

Their main objective is to streamline the development and deployment processes, improve scalability and reliability, and reduce time-to-market for applications running in the cloud.

A data engineer and a platform engineer both possess strong technical skills and work closely with various teams to ensure smooth operations within an organization. However, their focuses differ significantly. Data engineers concentrate on building and maintaining data-related infrastructure, enabling data scientists and analysts to derive insights and make data-driven decisions.

In contrast, platform engineers, often involved in cloud DevOps, manage a broader scope that includes the overall cloud infrastructure and application deployment processes. While data engineers primarily collaborate with data professionals, platform engineers work more closely with developers to streamline the software development lifecycle and ensure efficient, scalable, and reliable application performance. This delineation highlights the specialized yet complementary roles both play in advancing organizational technology goals.

Overall, while there may be some overlap in their responsibilities, data engineers and cloud DevOps professionals have distinct roles and skill sets, each contributing to the success of IT operations within organizations.

Summary

In today’s data-driven world, knowledge is a potent currency, and large companies are harnessing vast amounts of data to gain insights and drive strategic decisions. However, the process of turning this data into actionable knowledge is increasingly complex. While experience and domain expertise play crucial roles in understanding business practices, data engineering is now more critical than ever in extracting valuable insights from the overwhelming volume of data generated. Data engineering bridges the gap between raw data and meaningful insights, enabling organizations to leverage the full potential of their data to drive innovation, efficiency, and competitiveness in the modern market landscape.
